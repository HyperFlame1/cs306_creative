Background / Scenario

Background

Shauna Pauls is an Assistant System Administrator for Shenanigans Software, Inc.

Shenanigans Software, Inc. is a U.S. government contractor that provides software solutions to many branches of the federal government, including defense, homeland security, intelligence, and healthcare.

Scenario

In 2015, Shauna Pauls graduated college and soon landed a job at Shenanigans Software, Inc. When joining Shenanigans, Shauna's paperwork stated that she was to act as an Assistant System Administrator (which consisted of the following duties: managing continuous deployment of the processes and maintaining/installing/configuring/upgrading the servers) and perform in-office IT services for other employees on an as-needed basis, the same as her peers. Her starting salary was a generous but not unusual $28.00 an hour ($58,240 per year). However as ‘the new guy,’ all of these IT tasks were given to her so that the other System Administrators could focus on their corresponding duties. As Shauna was frequently required to work overtime to complete all the IT tasks, she asked to be paid an overtime rate but was told that as a System Administrator she was exempt from Fair Labor Standards special overtime rates.

Shauna is now suing Shenanigans, contending that the majority of her work is menial and non-exempt. She is demanding:

  • back-pay of overtime wages
  • legal fees
  • overtime pay for any overtime she works during the remainder of her employment at Shenanigans Software

Legal Questions

Is Shauna Pauls entitled to overtime pay, even when she was initially hired for a position that is exempt under the FLSA?

  • Is Pauls' position an administrative position and exempt under the FLSA?

    Supporting Case:
    Terri Jones & Beatrice Moon v. Virginia Oil Company, Inc.

Legal Elements

To prove that she is not exempt from overtime under the Fair Labor Standards Act, the plaintiff must show:

  1. Whether the plaintiff is considered a computer professional;
  2. Whether the plaintiff was defined as exempt by her employer;
  3. Whether the defendant properly defined the plaintiff as exempt;
  4. Whether the plaintiff would expect her type of work to render her exempt;
  5. Whether the FLSA is intended to protect those doing the type of work the plaintiff was majorly involved in;
  6. Whether the plaintiff is considered an "administrative" employee;
  7. No employer may be required to “pay an employee overtime compensation, for or on account of … (2) activities which are preliminary to or postliminary to said principal activity or activities” 29 U.S.C. §254(a)

The FLSA makes sure that non-salary employees get paid time and a half if they work for more than 40 hours in a work week. However, there are many exemptions to overtime pay. Administratrative, executive, and professional employees are all exempt, as well as "computer employees".

DOL:

  • Defines computer systems analyst, computer programmer, software engineer, or other similarly skilled worker as exempt from overtime compensation and details what the primary duties must be to be exempt - 29 USCS § 213(a)(17)
  • Defines executive, administrative, or professional workers as exempt from overtime compensation - FLSA § 213(a)(1)
  • Defines Computer systems analysts, computer programmers, software engineers or other similarly skilled workers in the computer field as professionals and therefore eligible for exemption - 29 CFR § 541.400(a)
  • Gives the short title as the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 - FLSA § 201
  • DOL: Defining and Delimiting the Exemptions for Executive, Administrative, Professional, Outside Sales and ComputerEmployees - LexisNexis
