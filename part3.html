Background

-Shenanigans Software's Backstory-

Scenario:

When Shauna Pauls began work at Shenanigans Software, Inc., her paperwork stated that she was to act as software developer and perform in-office IT services for other employees on an as-needed basis, the same as her peers. Her starting salary was a generous but not unusual $28.00 an hour ($58,240 per year) — Shenanigans is a Federal subcontractor with deep pockets. However as ‘the new guy,’ all of these IT tasks were given to her so that the other developers could focus on programming. As Shauna was frequently required to work overtime to complete all the IT tasks, she asked to be paid an overtime rate but was told that as a programmer she was exempt from Fair Labor Standards special overtime rates. Shauna then sued Shenanigans, contending that the majority of her work was menial and non-exempt.

Legal Elements

  1. Whether the plaintiff was defined as exempt by her employer;
  2. Whether the defendant properly defined the plaintiff as exempt;
  3. Whether the plaintiff would expect her type of work to render her exempt;
  4. Whether the FLSA is intended to protect those doing the type of work the plaintiff was majorly involved in;
  5. DOL:
    • FLSA § 213(a)(17)
    • FLSA § 213(a)(1)
    • 29 CFR § 541.400(a)
    • FLSA § 201
    • DOL: Defining and Delimiting the Exemptions for Executive, Administrative, Professional, Outside Sales and Computer Employees — LexisNexis
  6. No employer may be required to “pay an employee overtime compensation, for or on account of … (2) activities which are preliminary to or postliminary to said principal activity or activities” 29 U.S.C. §254(a)
