Background / Scenario

Background

In 2015, Shauna Pauls graduated college and soon landed a job at Shenanigans Software, Inc. Shenanigans is a U.S. government contractor who provides software solutions to many branches of the federal government, including defense, homeland security, intelligence, and healthcare. When joining Shenanigans, Shauna's paperwork stated that she was to act as an Assistant System Administrator (which consisted of the following duties: managing continuous deployment of the processes and maintaining/installing/configuring/upgrading the servers) and perform in-office IT services for other employees on an as-needed basis, the same as her peers. Her starting salary was a generous but not unusual $28.00 an hour ($58,240 per year). However as ‘the new guy,’ all of these IT tasks were given to her so that the other System Administrators could focus on their corresponding duties. As Shauna was frequently required to work overtime to complete all the IT tasks, she asked to be paid an overtime rate but was told that as a System Administrator she was exempt from Fair Labor Standards special overtime rates.

Shauna then sued Shenanigans, contending that the majority of her work was menial and non-exempt.

LexisNexis Case Bank (Choose only 8)

  1. Terri Jones & Beatrice Moon v. Virginia Oil Company, Inc.

    2. Jones v. VA Oil Company mainly deals with classifying the overtime exemption status of an employee upon describing the actual duties of an employee. The percentage of work time spent performing a certain task can help determine if that employee’s primary duties will classify them as an employee to which the overtime exemption statute can be applied. The decision made in the US Court of Appeals for the 4th Circuit holds precedent in our area, and it has been overturned neither by the court itself nor a higher court. The decision has been positively treated in cases that have cited it with most courts following the decision. However, some lower courts have utilized the decision in other contexts, but further research does not indicate that the decision has been misused. This case will help the defense with their argument in our case. The plaintiff in this case is employed in a managerial capacity, but they regularly perform duties that non-exempt employees perform. The decision shows that an employee can still be exempt from the overtime statute even if they perform extra duties that would normally be compensated with overtime pay for non-managerial employees. Thus, this could help the defense in our case possibly show that the exemption still applies.

  2. Tracy T. Yuen v. U.S. Asia Commercial Development Corp.

    3. Yuen v. U.S. Asia Commercial Development Corp. discusses whether an employee who occasionally performs the duties of an employee who is exempt from overtime pay still falls under the category of overtime exemption. This questions the classification of the employee based on the normal daily duties of said employee This case suggests that even if some duties that are considered exempt from overtime pay are performed by the employee, they still may be entitled to overtime pay if their main duties consist of work that is not overtime exempt. This decision was made in the US District Court for the Eastern District of Virginia, which holds precedence over our case. There have been no decisions made by a higher court to contradict this decision. Upon reviewing the cases that cited this decision, it appears that the decision has been positively used without mistreatment that would call its validity into question. This case should help the plaintiff’s side demonstrate that the extent of the plaintiff’s duties do not qualify them as a computer professional, even if they occasionally perform tasks that designate them as such. If the plaintiff’s side can show that the plaintiff’s primary duties would be done by an employee who can be compensated by overtime pay, then this case could prove to be useful.

  3. Howard E. Shockley, et al. v. City of Newport News

    4. Thirty-two current and former police officers brought this case against the City of Newport News in an attempt to obtain back-pay for overtime work. They claimed that the City had improperly classified them as salaried managing employees. While the Appeals Court affirmed that most of the officers were in fact managers and only due compensation due to former regulations, the Media Regulations Sergeants were found to have been mis-classified. While they were officers nominally paid on a salary, their primary duties were answering phones, talking to the press, and acting as censors for active cases. This, combined with 29 CFR § 541.400(a), could support the Plaintiff in arguing that her job title and primary job description alone should not exempt her from overtime pay. Instead, the actual work activities she performed on a day-to-day basis must be taken into account.

  4. Integrity Staffing Solutions, Inc. v. Jesse Busk, et al.

    5. ISS v. Busk is a unanimous United States Supreme Court decision that clarifies compensible work and work-related activities under the Portal-to-Portal Act (29 U.S.C. §254(a)). Former employees of ​ISS sued the company to obtain compensation for time spent in required security screenings after work each day. They claimed that as the screenings were required, they fell under “principal [work] activities;” the Supreme Court found that simply being required was not a sufficient test to define something as a principal activity. For example, battery plant employees donning protective clothing or meatpackers sharpening their knives would be compensible activities, as without performing them the employee’s activities would be impossible or at least severely hampered. On the other hand, poultry-plant employees waiting to don protective gear was very similar yet too far removed from the actual activities of their work. The SC held that ISS could have eliminated the screenings completely without impacting the worker’s ability to perform their jobs, and that only “work that the employee is ​ employed to perform [sic]” is compensible. The Defense may use this to argue that as Shauna’s IT tasks were not required for the execution of her main duty in performing system administration, that the IT tasks were non-principal. She would not be entitled to overtime pay as no employer may be required to “pay an employee overtime compensation, for or on account of … (2) activities which are preliminary to or postliminary to said principal activity or activities.” 29 U.S.C. §254(a).

  5. Icicle Seafoods, Inc. v. Worthington et al.

    6. This case revolves revolves around the classification of employees working on a barge at sea as “seamen”. Initially, Worthington et al. had brought suit against Icicle Seafoods, Inc. to recover overtime pay which they believed they were entitled to under the FLSA. The district court for the Western District of Washington held that these employees were exempt from overtime pay due to being “seamen” in 29 USCS § 213(b)(6). Worthington et al. appealed, and the appellate 9th Circuit Court of Appeals reversed the decision. The Court of Appeals believed that the respondents were not “seamen” but instead industrial maintenance employees on a non-moving barge. The case came to the Supreme Court, which then vacated and remanded the case back to the Court of Appeals. “How the respondents spent their working time on board the Arctic Star is a question of fact. The question whether their particular activities excluded them from the overtime benefits of the FLSA is a question of law”. Justice Stevens dissented, with the opinion that he would have affirmed the judgement of the 9th Circuit court, and “the facts are undisputed”. “[One] does not become a ‘seaman’ under the FLSA merely by performing services aboard a vessel on navigable waters.” In this case, Worthington et al. were assumed to be seamen because they worked at sea. Even though partial amounts of their work may have been classified as “maritime” and therefore something a “seaman” might do, their primary duties as industrial maintenance employees are nonexempt from overtime pay. The legal elements this case affects are whether the plaintiff was defined as exempt by their employer, and whether the employer properly defined this. It appears that the plaintiffs were indeed defined as exempt, but not properly defined, since it wasn’t very clear whether they were classified as “seamen” or as the industrial workers they were initially hired as. As for the current precedential status, the Supreme Court case has been cited a great many times, followed 19 times, and distinguished on three occasions. A question of fact vs. a question of law is a key quote in many of the citing cases. In our scenario, this helps the plaintiff Pauls. She can argue that just because she is associated with the software programmers, does not mean that her primary duty is doing software programming, and therefore her “particular activities” doing mostly IT support would not exclude her from overtime benefits.

  6. Nancy A. Williams v. Genex Services, LLC

    7. This case focuses on the classification of an employee as a “professional”, which makes them exempt from overtime pay. Nancy Williams, a registered nurse, worked for Genex Services as a field medical case manager (FMCM). She filed suit against Genex, claiming that Genex is required to pay her for overtime under the FLSA. Under the FLSA, “professional employees” are exempt from receiving overtime pay. She claimed that she spent more than fifty percent of her time while working performing menial tasks and that even a “lay person” could perform. And even though she may use her professional knowledge as a registered nurse on occasion, she does so much less than the fifty percent threshold set by § 541.700(b). However, the court found that the amount of time an employee spends doing exempt work does not indicate that the employee is a learned professional or not. “Time alone… is not the sole test”. Williams’ work record reflected that a very large majority of her work involved the application of her skills and knowledge as a registered nurse, and her resume indicated that she worked as a case manager. The court then notes that the FLSA was not designed to protect Williams due to her high salary ($80,000/y); it was intended to protect “low paid ‘rank and file’ employees.” This case actually affects all of the legal elements of our case. The plaintiff was indeed defined as exempt by her employer, who classified her as a “professional employee”. Genex defined this correctly; both the district and appellate courts ruled in Genex’s favor. The plaintiff expected that her type of work should be not exempt, claiming that she did non-exempt work greater than fifty percent of the time. The court event mentioned that the FLSA was intended to protect low paid employees, and not people with higher salaries and managerial positions such as Williams. ​ Williams v. Genex has been distinguished in one case, but was followed in another case and cited several times, so it appears that the case still holds merit. In our scenario, the defense could use this to help their side by showing that the amount of time spent doing nonexempt work does not change their classification as an exempt employee.

  7. Robert Schmidt v. Bartech Group, Inc., et al.

    8. Schmidt v. Bartech Group, Inc., et al. discusses whether an employee who is paid on hourly basis and who is exempt based on certain provisions of Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) can still be able to get paid for the overtime hours that he/she has worked. The case affects one of the legal elements in our proposed case that is FLSA exemptions. The decision of the case suggests that if an employee falls under the minimum wage and overtime pay provisions of the FLSA, then he/she is exempt from the overtime payments. According to the court's decision, the employee (plaintiff) failed to prove that he should have been paid for overtime hours because he did not have a claim for payment of his hourly wage for hours worked in excess of 40 hours per week. The decision of the case was ruled by the United States Court of Appeals For the Fourth Circuit, and it does not have any subsequent appellate history. It contains citing decisions with no analysis assigned. This case will help the defense side in our case. If the plaintiff (Shauna) in our case is an employee who works on a daily basis, she must provide evidence that she is exempt under FLSA provisions, which is “​ from both the minimum wage and overtime pay provisions due to the nature of her job”, such that she has a claim for payment of her hourly wage for hours worked in excess of 40 hours per week. Therefore, the defense in our proposed case could use this case's decision as a base to prove that the plaintiff (Shauna) is exempt from overtime payments.

  8. Gerard Morrison et al. v. County of Fairfax

    9. Morrison et al. v. County of Fairfax discusses whether the fire captains fall within an exception, which includes certain “executive” and “administrative” employees whose primary job duties were related to management, to the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) so that they were not entitled to overtime compensation. The case touches upon the following legal elements: FLSA exemptions and Exemption Status of Employee based on actual duties, which are described next. According to the outcome of the case, the County failed to provide enough evidence that the fire captains' primary duties included doing executive or administrative works and that they[fire captains] had spent majority of their time doing these types of duties. Similar to the firefighters of lower ranks, the fire captains were also required to perform the same daily duties: training, first responding, etc. The decision was established by the United States Court of Appeals For the Fourth Circuit after it reversed and remanded the district court's decision. The decision of the court on this case has been treated positively based on the citing decisions (4​th Circuit – Court of Appeals and 4​th​ Circuit – U.S. District Courts) that followed the case. This case relates to our case proposal, and it will help us with plaintiff's side (Shauna), as she was hired as a software developer and also perform IT services when only needed but was frequently required to do the IT stuff by working overtime. The case also shows that the FLSA exemptions are supposed to be “narrowly construed against the employer”, and that an employer must prove the application of an exemption by providing convincing evidence. And as IT duties do not fall under the provisions of FLSA exemptions, the plaintiff(Shauna) could use that to prove that she was supposed to get paid the overtime hours that she had worked.

  9. Douglas W. Luzik et al. v. Commonwealth of Virginia

    10. Luzik v Commonwealth is in regards to a group of over one hundred juvenile probation officers who are suing the Commonwealth of Virginia for allegedly not paying them overtime. It concerns itself with the burden of proof the Commonwealth must present that their employees are exempt by FLSA (Fair Labor Standards Act). The court trial case found that they were salaried and their duties were administrative which would make them exempt and owed no overtime pay. The Supreme Court vacated this judgement however, and remanded the case. They then found that the officers passed the Salary Basis Test, but fail the Duties Test. The latter was not properly evaluated at the trial court, but here they find that most of the officers do not have duties that can be defined as administrative or professional. The normal officers' tasks are not pertaining to management policies or general business operations and cannot fire or hire staff. They are not professional becauses they did not receive prolonged intellectual instruction or study. The judgement was later reversed, but this was due to the court's jurisdiction. They were unable to hear their claims. The Commonwealth of Virginia had not waived its sovereign immunity from the FLSA. This approaches the definition of “administrative” tasks being their best potential argument against being exempt under the Duties Tests: Administrative Exemption.

  10. Keith Michael Wise v. City of Norfolk

    11. The case involves the fire department chiefs and lieutenants whom argue that they are manual laborers and thus aren’t exempt, but the court rules that they fall under the Executive Exemption of the Duties Tests. The claim is that even though the majority of their duties may not be executive that they may spend a large portion of their time fighting fires, they are still acting in executive capacities. They can be performing managerial duties even "while physically doing something else." There was no subsequent appellate history. This helps the defendants argue against the plaintiff’s majority IT work. She is still a System Administrator.

Detailed Witness Statements

Client Witness for the Plaintiff — Shauna Pauls

Witness Details

Voluntary Information

I was hired by Shenanigans shortly after graduating college in 2015 as an Assistant Systems Administrator. I was told that the SysAdmins typically handled IT support for the rest of the office, which tended to interfere with their actual work, especially on crunch days. Crunch days are when software is pushed to production, or a database goes down, that kind of thing — the entire SysAdmin staff needs to be available. My job description was to perform systems administration tasks as directed and to perform office IT support on an as-needed basis. In my contract, I was also informed that as a “computer employee” I was exempt from overtime pay under the Fair Labor Standards Act.

Once I started, my supervisor, Michael Trump, told me that he would likely assign me most of the IT work; this made sense as it had been hinted that I was hired to take some of the pressure off the team. I ended up being instructed to perform my SysAdmin tasks in the morning, then make rounds through the office after my lunch break to see if anyone needed help. The company has an IT help desk website where people can request help, but most people don’t know how to use it so they just don’t. Because there are about 150 people in the office, these rounds would end up taking most of the afternoon, after which I would have a few remaining SysAdmin tasks to perform. Typically I’d average an hour of overtime per day on typical days, but that could go up to 4 hours on crunch days.

The IT tasks were things like making sure computers and other hardware were plugged in (power, video, sound, etc.), helping people set up two-factor authentication, finding “lost” or “deleted” files or emails, setting up accounts on spare computers for interns, cleaning keyboards, refilling paper & ink/toner in printers, and helping people set up wireless & printer access on their desktop computers.

Occasionally I’d complain online about how tiring my job was. After about a year, someone suggested that I might have been incorrectly classified as exempt from overtime pay. At this point I read the section in the FLSA, and it sounded like most of the stuff I was doing was menial tasks and didn’t really fit into the “computer employee” description. I mentioned this to Michael, but he just said I’d have to talk to HR about stuff like that.

On my next round I stopped by Stacy Johnson’s office over in HR and mentioned that I didn’t think the exemption applied to me. Stacy seemed distracted by other work and didn’t seem to want to talk about it, and hardly looked away from her work. She said there was nothing she could do; I was hired as a computer professional and was exempt. After a while it was clear I wasn’t making any headway. Frustrated, I emailed a lawyer that evening and set up an appointment.

Reluctant Information

Shauna spent a good portion of her rounds chatting with the people she was helping. While not technically objectionable under normal circumstances, the work was not done in the most efficient manner. In addition, the other SysAdmins also frequently work overtime; however this is done while performing actual systems administration tasks. Finally, when talking to Stacy Shauna became frustrated and raised her voice a little, threatening to sue the company before she had talked to a lawyer about the validity of her complaint.

Evidence

Helpful to the Plaintiff

  • Shauna recorded 10~15 hours of overtime per 2-week pay period
  • 40-60% of Shauna’s work involved office IT tasks, depending on the day
  • Although not strictly officially, Shauna was hired to alleviate the SysAdmin’s workload by taking over their IT tasks rather than as just another SysAdmin
  • The IT tasks performed by Shauna were very menial
  • The response from HR didn’t seem to actually consider Shauna’s argument
  • While other sysadmins also worked overtime, they were definitely exempt due to the tasks they were performing

Helpful to the Defense

  • Shauna’s IT work was mostly done after lunch break — before she started her “regularly assigned tasks” related to her job description
  • Could argue that if she was more efficient with her rounds she could have avoided overtime
  • Shauna was unfamiliar with the actual wording of the FLSA until a year into her job
  • Shauna was very new to the field, inexperienced in the workplace & employment laws
  • The other sysadmins also worked overtime
  • Shauna does not have a paper trail for initial question to HR (asked in-person rather than via email)

Client Witness for the Defense — Stacy Johnson

Witness Details

Statement

I joined Shenanigans Software, Inc. straight out of college after previously interning with the company the summer of my Junior year. When I was an intern, I mainly performed menial tasks such as making copies and brewing coffee for the full time employees, but I still learned the ropes of the job nonetheless. When I received an offer for a full time position following my graduation, I was somewhat reluctant to take the position because I did not want to be making coffee for the rest of my career. However, my hard work during the internship was recognized, and I was given the responsibility of helping manage the payroll for our employees. I have access to a database that logs all employee work hours so that I can properly compensate our hard working employees.

A few months ago, the employee in charge of hiring for ten years left the company after accepting an HR position at Boolgle, which left the HR department in dire need of someone who specialized in the hiring process. The head of the HR department, who was in charge of me during my internship, saw my potential as an excellent hiring manager despite my lack of experience. After receiving some general pointers from a few people who have had hiring experience, I was tasked with hiring the position of Assistant System Administrator, which had recently been created to alleviate the duties performed by our current System Administrators. After an extensive interviewing process, Shauna Pauls was chosen as our new Assistant System Administrator, since she was the most qualified for the position out of our applicants.

Shauna appeared to excel at her duties over the past few months and she received numerous compliments from employees in other departments. During the last pay cycle, Shauna requested a meeting with me about her current pay. After talking about how tired she was from working frequent overtime hours, she requested compensation for the overtime hours that she was working. Since I had experience with the inner workings of the employee payroll, I had payed many of our employees for their overtime hours, but only employees that were not “computer professionals” and employees in administrative positions. I told her that she was not eligible for overtime hours because she was technically a computer professional, which is exempt from overtime pay under the Fair Labor Standards Act. After telling her this, she became rather angry and threatened to sue our company to get her compensation. I told her that she was being unreasonable and that I was familiar with who can and cannot receive overtime pay, which caused her to storm out of my office while mumbling angrily to herself.

When I reported this to the head of the HR department, he was confident in my assessment of the situation and that nothing serious would arise from the situation.

Secret Information

Stacy is relatively computer illiterate and has no idea what any technical jargon actually means. She hired Shauna because her resume seemed rather impressive after boolgling a few of the technical terms and reading the Wikibedia pages. Stacy has personally received assistance from Shauna when she could not turn on her laptop, which was actually uncharged because she did not plug the cord into the outlet.

Extra: Stacy shuffled the stack of resumes and randomly pulled out Shauna’s resume for the selection process.

Evidence

Helpful to the Plaintiff

Being a recent college graduate, Stacy barely has any actual experience with her job, which may reduce the strength of her testimony. Shauna is the first employee that Stacy has hired, so she may have made some mistakes during the hiring process. Proving that Stacy has no useful knowledge of computers could alter Shauna’s classification as a computer professional.

Helpful to the Defense

Stacy is well aware of the exemptions in the FLSA, since she was responsible for paying all employees regardless of their exemption status. Stacy personally hired Shauna, so she knows the details of her contract and what tasks Shauna is expected to perform. She also has access to the hours worked by each employee, so she knows when someone has worked overtime.
